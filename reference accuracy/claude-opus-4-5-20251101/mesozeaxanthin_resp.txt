# Mesozeaxanthin: A Comprehensive Introduction

## Overview and Chemical Classification

Mesozeaxanthin (3R,3'S-zeaxanthin) is a xanthophyll carotenoid belonging to the terpenoid class of natural compounds. Like all carotenoids, it is a C40 tetraterpenoid synthesized from eight isoprene units. Chemically, mesozeaxanthin is a stereoisomer of zeaxanthin, distinguished by the configuration at the 3' carbon position. While zeaxanthin (3R,3'R-zeaxanthin) has both hydroxyl groups in the R configuration, mesozeaxanthin has an R configuration at C3 and an S configuration at C3', making it a meso compound—optically inactive due to internal symmetry (Bone et al., 2007).

## Occurrence and Biosynthesis

Unlike lutein and zeaxanthin, mesozeaxanthin is not commonly found in the typical human diet. It is present in only a few dietary sources, including certain fish species (particularly shrimp, trout, and sardines) and turtle fat (Johnson et al., 2005). However, the primary source of mesozeaxanthin in the human body appears to be through metabolic conversion from lutein within the retina (Johnson et al., 2005; Bone et al., 2007).

Research has demonstrated that mesozeaxanthin is formed in the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) through the enzymatic reduction of lutein. This conversion involves the migration of a double bond in the lutein molecule, converting the ε-ring to a β-ring while simultaneously inverting the stereochemistry at the 3' position (Shyam et al., 2017). Studies using deuterium-labeled lutein have confirmed this metabolic pathway in primates, establishing lutein as the precursor for retinal mesozeaxanthin (Johnson et al., 2005).

## Distribution in the Macular Pigment

Mesozeaxanthin is a critical component of the macular pigment (MP), the yellow pigment concentrated in the macula lutea of the human retina. The macular pigment consists of three carotenoids: lutein, zeaxanthin, and mesozeaxanthin, collectively referred to as the macular xanthophylls (Bernstein et al., 2016).

The distribution of these three carotenoids within the macula is not uniform. Mesozeaxanthin is predominantly concentrated in the very center of the fovea, where it can constitute up to 50% of the total macular carotenoid content. Zeaxanthin predominates in the parafoveal region, while lutein is distributed throughout the peripheral macula (Bone et al., 1997). This specific spatial arrangement suggests distinct functional roles for each carotenoid in macular protection (Bernstein et al., 2016).

## Biological Functions and Mechanisms

### Blue Light Filtration

One of the primary functions of mesozeaxanthin, along with the other macular xanthophylls, is the absorption of high-energy blue light (wavelengths 400-500 nm). The absorption maximum of mesozeaxanthin occurs at approximately 486-489 nm in organic solvents, making it highly effective at filtering potentially damaging short-wavelength visible light before it reaches the photoreceptors (Bone et al., 1997). This pre-receptoral filtering mechanism is thought to reduce photooxidative stress and minimize chromatic aberration, thereby enhancing visual acuity (Hammond & Fletcher, 2012).

### Antioxidant Properties

Mesozeaxanthin exhibits potent antioxidant activity, which is critical in the highly oxidative environment of the retina. The retina has the highest metabolic rate of any tissue in the body and is continuously exposed to light, making it particularly susceptible to oxidative damage (Bernstein et al., 2016).

Studies comparing the antioxidant efficacy of the three macular carotenoids have demonstrated that mesozeaxanthin possesses superior antioxidant activity. Cantrell et al. (2003) demonstrated that mesozeaxanthin is the most effective quencher of singlet oxygen among the macular xanthophylls. Additionally, Li et al. (2010) reported that mesozeaxanthin showed the greatest ability to scavenge reactive oxygen species and protect membrane lipids from peroxidation compared to lutein and zeaxanthin.

## Clinical Significance

### Age-Related Macular Degeneration

The role of macular carotenoids in protecting against age-related macular degeneration (AMD) has been extensively investigated. AMD is the leading cause of irreversible blindness in developed countries among individuals over 50 years of age (Bernstein et al., 2016).

Several studies have reported that individuals with AMD tend to have lower macular pigment optical density (MPOD) compared to healthy controls, suggesting that reduced macular carotenoid levels may be a risk factor for disease development (Beatty et al., 2001). The AREDS2 study demonstrated that lutein and zeaxanthin supplementation could reduce the progression to advanced AMD in certain patient populations (Age-Related Eye Disease Study 2 Research Group, 2013).

More recent research has focused on formulations containing all three macular carotenoids. The MOST (Macular Pigment and Serum Response to Meso-Zeaxanthin, Lutein and Zeaxanthin Supplementation) and COMPASS (Carotenoids in Age-Related Maculopathy Italian Study) studies demonstrated that supplementation with mesozeaxanthin in combination with lutein and zeaxanthin effectively increases MPOD and may provide enhanced protection compared to formulations lacking mesozeaxanthin (Nolan et al., 2012; Piermarocchi et al., 2012).

### Visual Function

Beyond structural protection, macular carotenoids have been associated with improvements in various aspects of visual performance. Studies have reported that higher MPOD correlates with better contrast sensitivity, reduced glare disability, and faster photostress recovery (Hammond & Fletcher, 2012). Supplementation studies with carotenoid formulations including mesozeaxanthin have demonstrated improvements in these visual function parameters in both healthy subjects and those with early AMD (Nolan et al., 2016).

## Supplementation and Bioavailability

Mesozeaxanthin supplements are typically derived from marigold flowers (Tagetes erecta) through a conversion process or produced synthetically. Studies have confirmed that supplemental mesozeaxanthin is bioavailable and can increase both serum carotenoid levels and MPOD (Nolan et al., 2012).

Thurnham et al. (2015) demonstrated that mesozeaxanthin from supplements is absorbed and incorporated into the retina, with measurable increases in MPOD observed within weeks of supplementation initiation. The bioavailability of mesozeaxanthin is enhanced when consumed with dietary fat, consistent with its lipophilic nature.

## Safety Profile

Mesozeaxanthin has been evaluated for safety in multiple toxicological studies and has been granted Generally Recognized as Safe (GRAS) status by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration. Chronic toxicity studies in animals have demonstrated no adverse effects at doses far exceeding typical human consumption, and clinical trials have reported no significant side effects associated with mesozeaxanthin supplementation (Xu et al., 2013).

## Conclusion

Mesozeaxanthin is a unique macular carotenoid that plays a crucial role in protecting the central retina from light-induced damage and oxidative stress. Its preferential accumulation at the foveal center, combined with its superior antioxidant properties, suggests a specialized protective function in the area of highest visual acuity. The ongoing research into mesozeaxanthin's role in eye health continues to support its importance as both a biomarker of macular health and a potential therapeutic agent for conditions such as AMD.
