Calamanene is a sesquiterpenoid compound with diverse origins and identified biological activity, as documented across multiple sources. Structurally, it is classified as a C15 bicyclic sesquiterpenoid [1], and is further characterized as an aromatic sesquiterpenoid that often co-occurs with related compounds such as cadalene, cuparene, and curcumene [2][3][4]. While typically present in low concentrations in some contexts [5], it can be the most abundant product in certain samples, such as those from fossil and extant resins [1].

In terms of biological sources, calamanene is primarily associated with higher plants, where it is recognized as a higher plant biomarker, though some sesquiterpenoids may also have algal origins [6]. Specific higher plant sources include cannabis, isolated from mature flower tops of three cannabis varieties (Bedrocan®, Bedrobinol®, and Bediol®) [7], and the Dipterocarpaceae family, particularly the genus Shorea, where it is detected in high abundance in both extant and fossil resins [1][8]. Additionally, calamanene and its isomers are found in marine organisms, specifically soft corals of the genus Sarcophyton. For example, trans-calamanene has been isolated from Sarcophyton glaucum (noted as the first report of this compound from a soft coral) [9] and Sarcophyton ehrenbergi [10], and is among the sesquiterpenes identified across 16 Sarcophyton species examined between 1995 and 2011 [11]. Cis/trans-calamanene is also present in the leaves of the plant Endiandra baillonii [12].

Regarding biological activity, trans-calamanene exhibits mild cytotoxicity, with a GI50 value of approximately 10 µM against P-338, A-549, HT-29, and MEL-28 tumor cells [9]. This activity highlights a potential functional role of the compound beyond its significance as a biomarker or structural component of its host organisms.
