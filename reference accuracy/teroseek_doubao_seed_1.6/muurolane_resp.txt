Muurolane is a class of sesquiterpenoid carbon skeletons widely distributed across diverse biological sources and synthesized via specific biogenetic pathways, with distinct structural subtypes and biological activities reported in the literature.

Structural Classification and Prevalence
Muurolane is identified as a sesquiterpenoid carbon skeleton, belonging to the larger class of sesquiterpenes (C₁₅H₂₄ derivatives) [1]. It is noted as one of the most common sesquiterpenoid carbon skeletons, represented by compounds such as bicyclosesquiphellandrene, α-muurolene, and germacrene D/γ-muurolene (GDγM) [2]. Within saturated fractions of natural extracts, muurolane co-occurs with other sesquiterpenoid classes including nordrimane, elemane, eudesmane, and cadinane [3], while it is also among the widely distributed sesquiterpenoid classes in conifer families alongside cadinanes, copaanes, and caryophyllanes [4]. Specific structural subtypes include muurolane-2β,9β-diol-3-ene [5][6] and 5,10-epoxymuurolane—a cyclic ether or oxygenated cyclic sesquiterpene [7][8].

Biological Sources
Muurolane-type sesquiterpenes are isolated from a range of organisms, spanning plants, gorgonians, and pyrolyzed materials. Plant sources include the stems and roots of C. praecox Link [5], Bupleurum scorzonerifolium (where aridity facilitates higher muurolane content) [9], Eremophila sturtii [10], Malvaceae (as a characteristic unusual sesquiterpene) [11], Landolphia dulcis var. barteri (as a new muurolane derivative) [12], and Cyperus articulatus [6]. Among non-plant sources, 5,10-epoxymuurolane is found exclusively in the gorgonians Gorgonia ventalina and Gorgonia flabellum [7], and in significant quantities in the chemical profiles of unspecified gorgonians [8]. Additionally, muurolanes are detected in the aliphatic fraction of Abies pinsapo pyrolyzed with LiAlH₄ [13].

Biosynthesis and Synthesis
Germacrene D serves as a key biogenetic precursor to muurolane-type sesquiterpenes. Under acidic conditions, germacrene D isomerizes to form muurolane alongside cadinane and amorphane type sesquiterpenes [14]. An alternative pathway involves protonation of (-)-germacrene D, leading to the formation of muurolane, cadinane, cubebane, and copaane skeletons [15]. Stereoisomerization of the germacrene cation, followed by cyclization at the C1 position, yields the intermediate muurolane [16]. Consistently, essential oils rich in germacrene D are typically accompanied by muurolane sesquiterpenoids, supporting germacrene D’s role as a precursor [17].

Biological Activity
Muurolane-2β,9β-diol-3-ene exhibits weak antibacterial activity against Staphylococcus aureus (ATCC 6538), Escherichia coli (ATCC 11775), and Pseudomonas aeruginosa (ATCC 10145) with minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) values ranging from 158–249 µg·mL⁻¹ [5]. It also shows activity against Candida glabrata (ATCC 2001) and S. aureus (ATCC 43300) with MIC values of 128–197 µg·mL⁻¹ [5].
