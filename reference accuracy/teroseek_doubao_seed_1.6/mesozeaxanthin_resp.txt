Structural Classification
Mesozeaxanthin (MZ) is consistently classified as a tetraterpenoid belonging to the xanthophyll carotenoid subclass, often grouped with lutein and zeaxanthin (collectively termed macular pigments) due to their roles in ocular health [1][2][3][4][5][6][7][8][9].

Origins
Commercial sources of MZ include carotenoid-rich flowers (marigold, calendula) and single-celled photosynthetic algae (Dunaliella), which are used in developing nutraceuticals and functional foods [2]. Additionally, MZ can be present in hen-derived MZ-enriched eggs, though non-enriched eggs lack this compound [3]. Dietary supplements are another origin, as patients may consume commercial supplements containing MZ [7].

Biological Mechanisms
MZ exerts protective effects through multiple pathways. It inhibits oxidative stress (OS) and endoplasmic reticulum stress (ERS) [1]. Specifically, MZ downregulates key ERS markers: 78 kDa glucose-regulated protein (GRP78), phosphorylated protein kinase RNA-like endoplasmic reticulum kinase (p-PERK), activating transcription factor 6 (ATF6), activating transcription factor 4 (ATF4), and phosphorylated c-Jun N-terminal kinase (p-JNK) [1]. When combined with lutein and zeaxanthin (L/Zi), MZ also downregulates these ERS markers and p-JNK, and its OS regulation may involve MAPK and Nrf2 pathways [1].

Applications
MZ has diverse applications in health and industry. It protects retinal health: L/Zi preserve photoreceptor function and morphology against light damage, including high-intensity blue light-induced degeneration [1]. MZ is part of macular pigment (MP), and supplementation with L/Zi/MZ increases MP optical density (MPOD) in healthy subjects [9]. It is used in nutraceuticals, functional foods, and supplements (as oil-filled soft gels or powder-filled capsules, though powder-filled supplements may have lower MZ concentrations due to oxidative degradation) [2][4]. MZ also serves as an adjunctive therapy for age-related macular degeneration (AMD), as declining macular xanthophylls (including MZ) contribute to AMD progression, justifying its use in clinical algorithms [5]. Additionally, prescriptive MZ repletion (with lutein/zeaxanthin and n-3 fats) is evaluated for impacts on retinal thickness and visual function [6].
