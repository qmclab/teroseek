Structural Classification
Longipinane and its derivatives are recognized as a distinct subclass of sesquiterpenoids, with longipinane-type and ent-longipinane-type variants distinguished by the position of a methyl group: longipinane-type sesquiterpenoids bear a methyl group at the C11 position, while ent-longipinane-type sesquiterpenoids have a methyl group at the C9 position [1]. Longipinane is classified as a tricyclic sesquiterpene [2] and is frequently identified as a constituent of essential oils across various plant species [3,5,6,7,8,9,14,15]. It is included in comprehensive reviews of natural sesquiterpenoid classes [12,14,15] and is noted to occur as poliesterified derivatives in some taxa [2].

Natural Occurrence
Longipinane-type and ent-longipinane-type sesquiterpenoids are widely distributed across plant families. Within liverworts (Marchantiophyta), both subtypes have been isolated from species including Marsupella emarginara, Gymnomitrion concinnatum, and Scapania undulaba [1]. Additionally, six new ent-longipinane-type sesquiterpenoids (marsupellins A–F) were isolated from the Chinese liverwort Marsupella alpine (also referenced as Marsupella alpina) in 2014 [1]. Longipinane itself has been detected in diverse angiosperms: Achillea styriaca [3], Achillea pannonica [3], Cyperus rotundus (as both longipinane and α-longipinane) [4,5], Origanum majorana L. (sweet marjoram) [4], Syneilesis palmata [5], Zygophyllum album [6], and Plagiochasma rupestre [7]. It is also a common secondary metabolite in Stevia species [10,11] and has been reported in gymnosperms of the Pinaceae, Cupressaceae s.str., and Podocarpaceae families [8].

Pharmacological Activities
Both longipinane-type and ent-longipinane-type sesquiterpenoids exhibit a broad spectrum of biological effects, including anticholinesterasic, antiaddiction, and antitumor activities [1]. Initial biological studies indicate that ent-longipinane-type sesquiterpenoids from liverworts (e.g., Marsupella alpina) possess moderate acetylcholinesterase (AChE) inhibitory activity [1,2], a property that highlights their potential for neuroprotective applications and drug development [9]. This AChE inhibitory activity distinguishes ent-longipinane-type sesquiterpenoids from other liverwort-derived metabolites such as muscle-relaxant marchantin-type cyclic bisbibenzyls [9]. α-Longipinane, a derivative from Cyperus rotundus, contributes to the plant’s therapeutic properties and is used in medicinal formulations [10].
