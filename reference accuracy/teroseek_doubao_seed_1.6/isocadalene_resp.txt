Isocadalene is a naphthalene derivative with a sesquiterpenoid skeleton, belonging to the cadalene-based aromatic sesquiterpenoid class alongside compounds such as cadalene, ionene, calamene, and 5,6,7,8-tetrahydrocadalene [1][2][3][4][5][6]. Its structural identity is confirmed by its classification as a cadinane-type sesquiterpene, with its formation linked to multiple biosynthetic and geochemical pathways.

Biosynthetic and Geochemical Origins
Isocadalene has diverse biological sources. Terrestrial higher plants, specifically Angiospermae, are a primary origin, where β-amyrin produced by Angiospermae acts as a precursor for alkylnaphthalenes including isocadalene [1]. Additionally, isocadalene has been isolated from the acetone extract of Heterotheca inuloides (H. inuloides) flowers, alongside related sesquiterpenes such as 4-methoxy-isocadalene [7][8][9]. It is also associated with gymnosperms of the Cupressaceae family, particularly Taxodium spp., where it co-occurs with other sesquiterpenoids (e.g., calamenene, calamene) and diterpenoids [2].

Formation Mechanisms
The synthesis of isocadalene involves multiple pathways. Geochemically, it forms via aromatization, isomerization, and dealkylation of cadinane-type sesquiterpenoids [10]. In sedimentary environments, isocadalene can also arise from isomerization and methylation reactions of cadalene catalyzed by montmorillonite [11]. Biosynthetically, β-amyrin from Angiospermae serves as a precursor for alkylnaphthalenes like isocadalene [1].

Applications as a Biomarker
Isocadalene, as part of the naphthalene derivatives with sesquiterpenoid skeletons, contributes to biomarker applications. While the 1,2,5,6-TeMN compound is explicitly noted as a potential indicator of Angiospermae-derived organic matter and immature coals [1], isocadalene’s association with Angiospermae precursors and specific plant families (Cupressaceae, H. inuloides) suggests it may support similar paleobotanical and maturity inferences. Additionally, its abundance relative to cadalene (e.g., in Cepu Block oil) indicates moderate to high maturity of organic matter [12].

Related Compounds
Derivatives of isocadalene, such as 4-methoxy-isocadalene, have been isolated from H. inuloides and used in semisynthetic reactions to produce cadinene analogs (e.g., (1S,4R)-7-hydroxycalamenane) [7]. Isocadalene also co-occurs with other cadalene-based sesquiterpenoids (e.g., tetrahydrocadalene, cadina-1(10),6,8-triene) in aromatic hydrocarbon fractions of ambers and sedimentary organic matter [4][5][6].
