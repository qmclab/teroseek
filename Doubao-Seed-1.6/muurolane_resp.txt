### Introduction to Muurolane Terpenoids  
Muurolanes are a structurally distinct subclass of sesquiterpenoids (C15 terpenoids) characterized by a tricyclic decalin core fused to a four-membered cyclobutane ring, with the parent structure (muurolane) defined by the IUPAC name (1aR,4aR,7R,7aR,7bS)-1a,4a,5,6,7,7a,7b,8-octahydro-1,1,4,7-tetramethylcyclopropa[e]azulene (or the equivalent hydrocarbon skeleton) [1]. Their biosynthesis originates from the universal sesquiterpene precursor farnesyl diphosphate (FPP), which undergoes cyclization via class I or II sesquiterpene synthases; for muurolanes, this typically involves initial cyclization of FPP to germacrene A, followed by protonation-induced rearrangements and cyclopropanation to form the signature tricyclic framework [2].  

Muurolanes are widely distributed across terrestrial and marine organisms, with notable sources including higher plants (e.g., *Artemisia* spp., *Zingiber officinale*), fungi (e.g., *Penicillium* and *Aspergillus* genera), and marine invertebrates (e.g., sponges of the *Dysidea* genus) [3]. In plants, they often contribute to chemical defense against herbivores and pathogens: for example, muurolane-4,10-diol from *Artemisia annua* exhibits dose-dependent antifeedant activity against the cotton bollworm (*Helicoverpa armigera*) [4]. Fungal muurolanes, such as 11-hydroxy-10-oxomuurol-4-en-3-one from *Penicillium chrysogenum*, have been linked to competition with competing microbial taxa via antimicrobial activity [5].  

Biologically, muurolanes display diverse bioactivities that have attracted pharmaceutical interest. Many exhibit anti-inflammatory properties: 7α-hydroxy-4α,10β-dimethyl-1,1,7α-trimethyl-1,2,3,4,5,6,7,8-octahydroazulene-4α-carboxylic acid (a muurolane derivative) inhibits nitric oxide (NO) production in lipopolysaccharide-stimulated RAW 264.7 macrophages by downregulating NF-κB signaling [6]. Anticancer activity is another key trait: the marine muurolane sesquiterpenoid dysidiolide, isolated from *Dysidea etheria*, acts as a potent inhibitor of cyclin-dependent kinase 2 (CDK2), a critical regulator of cell cycle progression, making it a lead compound for anticancer drug development [7]. Additionally, some muurolanes (e.g., muurolene oxide from *Zingiber officinale*) have demonstrated antioxidant activity by scavenging 2,2-diphenyl-1-picrylhydrazyl (DPPH) radicals [8].  

Despite their potential, muurolanes face challenges in translational research: their low natural abundance (often <0.1% of crude extracts) limits large-scale isolation, and their complex tricyclic structure complicates chemical synthesis [9]. Recent advances in metabolic engineering, such as heterologous expression of muurolane synthases from *Artemisia annua* in *Saccharomyces cerevisiae*, have shown promise for scalable production [10]. Future research will likely focus on optimizing biosynthetic pathways, exploring structure-activity relationships (SARs) to enhance bioactivity, and evaluating in vivo efficacy of lead muurolane derivatives [3].
