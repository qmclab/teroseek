
Fenchane is a class of monoterpenoid compounds with diverse structural derivatives, synthetic pathways, and biological and chemical applications, as detailed in the provided references.

Structurally, fenchane is classified as a monoterpene, often grouped with related monoterpenoid classes such as isocamphane and bornane [1][2][3][4]. It is naturally occurring: for example, cultivated E. microcorys leaves contain fenchane, with high contents detected in samples collected in February, March, July, and August [2]. Fenchane-based monoterpenoids include specific derivatives such as (-)-3-methylene fenchane (XIV), 2a-ethynyl-2b-hydroxyfenchane, 2-endo-chlorofenchane, and (2R)-fenchane-8,2-sultam [1][5][6][7]. Notably, fenchane units exhibit large steric demand, as observed in the crystal structure of the chlorophosphite BIFOP–Cl (1), which contributes to the unusually high stability of such compounds by limiting nucleophilic access to phosphorus atoms [8][9].

Synthetically, fenchane derivatives are accessed through various routes. (-)-3-methylene fenchane (XIV) is prepared via a Wittig reaction from (+)-camphor (98% purity, Aldrich) [1]. (2R)-fenchane-8,2-sultam ((-)-5a) and its derivatives (e.g., N-methyl- and N-acryloylfenchane-8,2-sultams (+)-5b,c, and N,N'-fumaroylbis[fenchane-8,2-sultam] (+)-5e) are synthesized from (1R)-fenchone (+)-2; these derivatives have been studied for their reactivity and diastereoselectivity as chiral auxiliaries, with comparisons made to (+)-1a (a bornane-10,2-sultam derivative) via Diels-Alder reactions with cyclopenta-1,3-diene under TiCl₄-mediated and uncatalyzed conditions [5]. 2a-ethynyl-2b-hydroxyfenchane is derived from (-)-fenchone, and its synthesis involves a reaction with copper(II) acetate in pyridine/methanol, followed by extraction and recrystallization [6]. Additionally, 2-endo-chlorofenchane is formed as a minor product (ca. 1:10 ratio with 2-endo-chlorobornane) via ionic hydrogen chloride addition to (1S)-α-pinene [7]. For hydrosilylation reactions of (-)-3-methylene fenchane (XIV), H₂PtCl₆ catalysis with HSiMe₂Cl or HSiMeCl₂ leads to a clean isocamphane skeleton rearrangement prior to hydrosilylation, yielding mixtures of [(2R)-endo]- and [(2S)-exo]-(2-bornane)methyl silanes (3a and 3b, respectively) in high yields; no hydrosilylation occurs with HSiCl₃ [1].

Applications and biological relevance of fenchane include its use as a CAP group in synthesized hydroxamic acids (15 and 25), where fenchane fragments (alongside adamantane and camphane) are incorporated via linkers with amide groups [10]. Fenchane-based hydroxamic acids are also noted for potential utility in Alzheimer’s disease (AD) treatment [10]. In plant systems, high fenchane contents in E. microcorys leaves correlate with increased foliar nutrients (Cu, Zn, Ca, P, K) and reduced total phenols, hydrolysable tannins, and Mg levels, as linked to RDA axis 2 values [2].

Furthermore, fenchane is part of broader monoterpenoid research: benchmark DFT and high-level ab initio calculations have been performed to identify reliable methods for studying terpenes, including fenchane monoterpenoids [11]. In amber analysis, fenchane is a constituent of the low-molecular-mass (LMM) components of Baltic and Romanian amber, alongside bornane, cymenes, and sesquiterpenoids [4]. The absolute configuration (AC) of the monoterpene fenchane (11) has been established via chemical correlations [12].
