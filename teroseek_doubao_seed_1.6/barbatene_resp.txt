Based on the provided documents, barbatene is a class of tricyclic sesquiterpenoids [1] that includes two primary isomers: α-barbatene and β-barbatene. These compounds are widely distributed across diverse taxa, including fungi, plants, liverworts, amoebae, and social amoebae, and exhibit distinct biosynthetic pathways, ecological roles, and potential applications.

Taxonomic Distribution
Barbatene isomers are produced by a broad range of organisms. Fungi are a major source: α-barbatene and β-barbatene have been identified in basidiomycetes such as Fomitopsis pinicola [2], Omphalotus olearius [2], Tricholoma vaccinum [3], Stereum hirsutum [4], Piptoporus betulinus [5], Flammulina velutipes [6][7], Trametes hirsuta [8], Armillaria ostoyae [9], and Fusarium graminearum [10][11], as well as ascomycetes like Fusarium poae [9][11]. Liverworts are another rich source, with both isomers detected in species including Marchantia polymorpha [12][13], Asterella marginata [12], Scapania aspera [14], Bazzania japonica [15], Plagiochila asplenioides [16], and Metacalypogeia alternifolia [17]. Vascular plants also produce barbatene: α-barbatene is found in Arabidopsis thaliana [18][19][20], Callitris sulcata [21][22][23][24], and Meum athamanticum [25], while β-barbatene occurs in Callitris sulcata [21][22][23][24], Solanum tuberosum [26], and Anemia tomentosa [27]. Additionally, β-barbatene has been identified in social amoebae (Dictyostelium discoideum [28][29]) and amoebae [30].

Biosynthesis
Barbatene biosynthesis is primarily catalyzed by sesquiterpene synthases (STS) via cyclization of precursor molecules. Key synthases for α-barbatene include Omp9 (from Omphalotus olearius) [2][31][32], g1826 and g1880 (clade IV) [3], PcSTS06 [31], AtBS (Arabidopsis α-barbatene synthase) [18], trichodiene synthase [33], Fla11 (from Flammulina velutipes) [7], and TPS11 [34]. For β-barbatene, critical synthases are Omp9 [2][32], g1826 and g1880 (clade IV) [3], PcSTS06 [31], ShSTS1 [31], FvSTS08 (from Flammulina velutipes) [6], Stehi1j159379 (from Stereum hirsutum) [4], DdTPS7/9 (from Dictyostelium discoideum) [28][29], β-barbatene synthase [4], PvTPS5 [35], and DfMTPSL4 [36].

The biosynthetic pathway involves cyclization of precursor molecules: α-barbatene and β-barbatene are synthesized from (E,E)-farnesyl pyrophosphate (FPP) via Omp9 [2][32] and PcSTS06 [31], respectively. Clade IV STSs initiate 1,6-ring closure using (3R)-nerolidyl pyrophosphate (NPP) to form the (6R)-β-bisabolol cation intermediate [6], while DdTPS9 selectively transforms (S)-NPP into β-barbatene [29]. In fungi, clade IV STSs catalyze 1,6 cyclization of 3R/S-nerolidyl pyrophosphate [3], and barbatene can also be derived from the bisabolyl cation [33][37][38].

Ecological and Industrial Applications
Barbatene exhibits diverse ecological functions: β-barbatene attracts insects for spore dispersal in fungi like Fomitopsis pinicola [28] and amoebae [30], and responds to herbivore infestation [6]. It also plays a role in plant-insect communication, as its release is modulated by herbivore feeding [39] and varies in PVYNTN-infected potato plants, affecting larval weight gain [26]. Additionally, β-barbatene is a marker for early detection of dry bubble disease in Agaricus bisporus [40].

Industrial applications of barbatene include its use as a high-energy aviation fuel (α-barbatene, ranked second in volumetric energy density among tested sesquiterpenes) [6][41], antimicrobial agents (both isomers in Scapania aspera extracts) [14][42], and acaricides (β-barbatene in Callitris sulcata oil, which shows 100% mortality against Rhipicephalus microplus larvae) [23].
